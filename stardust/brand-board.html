Visual Direction

Photography & Imagery

Style Direction

Moody, warm, high-contrast. Close-up food shots with shallow depth of field. Warm tungsten lighting with deep shadows. Industrial textures — exposed brick, marble counters, weathered steel. Authentic kitchen action, not staged perfection.

Do

  • Always warm white balance — no cool/blue tones
  • Show texture: bubbling cheese, charred crust, flour-dusted surfaces
  • Include environmental shots: the oven mouth glowing, hands stretching dough
  • People shots are candid — staff at work, guests mid-bite, never posed
  • Depth of field: subject sharp, background soft and warm

Don't

  • No sterile overhead flat-lays
  • No bright, evenly-lit food photography
  • No stock photo aesthetic — nothing that looks mass-produced
  • No cold blue or fluorescent lighting

Social Media

Instagram-first: close-ups of the pizza-making process, oven shots, ingredient details. Stories show behind-the-scenes kitchen moments.

Brand Voice

Voice & Character

"Confident, warm, and unpretentious. We know our craft inside out but never lecture. Like your favorite Italian uncle who takes food seriously but doesn't take himself seriously."

Passionate Knowledgeable Warm Direct Authentic
Pretentious Fussy Corporate Trendy Apologetic

Voice Examples

Do
"72 hours. That's how long our dough ferments before it ever sees the oven."

Leads with the compelling detail, lets the craft speak for itself

Don't
"We are proud to offer an artisanal dining experience featuring locally-sourced ingredients."

Corporate buzzwords — 'artisanal', 'dining experience', 'locally-sourced' are empty calories

Do
"San Marzano DOP. Not San Marzano style. Not San Marzano inspired. The real thing."

Direct, confident, makes the sourcing standard clear without being preachy

Don't
"Our award-winning chef has crafted a menu that celebrates the rich tapestry of Neapolitan tradition."

Overwrought and pretentious — we show, we don't brag

Do
"Wood-fired at 900°F. Sixty seconds. That's all it takes when the dough is right."

Short punchy sentences convey mastery and confidence

Don't
"Pizza is life! Come get your slice!!!"

Too casual, too many exclamation marks — we're warm, not wacky

Do
"Come hungry. Leave happy. It's not complicated."

Warm, inviting, no-nonsense

Hard Rules

  • No more than one exclamation mark per page
  • Never use 'artisanal', 'curated', or 'elevated'
  • Italian terms are fine when they're the real name (DOP, fior di latte, Margherita) — never for decoration
  • Short sentences. Let the periods do the work.
  • Always concrete over abstract — '900°F for 60 seconds' beats 'perfectly cooked'

Brand Voice

Tone Adaptation

Tone shifts by context but always stays warm and grounded. On the homepage we're inviting and confident. On the menu we're direct and appetizing. On About Us we're storytelling — personal, a little nostalgic, deeply passionate.

To Invite

Make people hungry and curious. Lead with sensory details — the smell of the oven, the sound of the crust, the sight of bubbling mozzarella.

To Inform

Share the craft without lecturing. Our sourcing, our process, our standards — stated plainly, not defensively.

To Connect

Build a relationship. We're a neighborhood place. We know your name, your order, your kids.

Clever vs. Clear

Be Clever

Menu item descriptions can have personality. Section headings can be evocative ('From the Oven' not 'Pizza Menu').

Be Clear

Hours, location, allergen info, ordering instructions — always crystal clear, never buried in prose.
