Typographic Rules

  • Headlines use Oswald Bold uppercase for maximum editorial impact
  • Body text uses Source Sans 3 Regular at 18px/1.6 for readability
  • Never use more than two font sizes in a single content block
  • Pull quotes use Oswald Medium italic when available
Photography & Video

Visual Direction

Warm, saturated, intimate. Shot in the converted shotgun house — velvet textures, neon glow, candlelight. People laughing, toasting, crying happy tears. Polaroid wall aesthetic. Golden hour and moody interior lighting.

Do

  • Always warm color grade — no cool/clinical tones
  • Favor candid moments over posed portraits
  • Include environmental details: velvet furniture, neon signs, cocktails, flowers
  • Shallow focus on subjects, bokeh on neon/candles
  • Diversity in subjects: age, ethnicity, body type, relationship configuration

Don't

  • No stark white or clinical lighting
  • No traditional funeral imagery — no caskets, hearses, or black veils
  • No stock-photo-perfect people in sterile environments
  • No desaturated or muted color palettes
Brand Voice

Voice & Language

Your wise, witty friend who brings champagne to the hard conversations. Warm but never saccharine. Direct but never cold. We talk about death the way we talk about life — with honesty, humor, and a cocktail in hand.

Irreverent Warm Direct Celebratory Honest Morbid Somber Clinical Preachy Euphemistic
"Plan your party. Yes, that one."

Concise, knowing, inviting — doesn't shy from the subject

"We provide end-of-life celebration facilitation services."

Corporate jargon strips all warmth and personality

"You have opinions about your funeral playlist. We're here for it."

Meets people where they are with humor and warmth

"Don't be afraid of death! 💀🎉"

Too flippant — trivializes the emotional weight

"Grief is just love with nowhere to go. Let's give it a dance floor."

Poetic but grounded — elevates without being precious

"In this difficult time, we are here for you."

Generic sympathy card language — exactly what we're replacing

"We've helped 200+ people throw the party of a lifetime. Their lifetime."

Confident, slightly cheeky, backed by credibility

"YOLO so plan your funeral NOW!"

Crass urgency — disrespects the gravity of the decision

Hard Rules

  • Never use the word "passed" — say "died" or "dead" with respect
  • Never use funeral industry euphemisms ("remains", "the deceased", "interment")
  • Humor should punch up, never down
  • Always center the person, not the service
  • It's okay to be funny. It's not okay to be flippant.
Tone Adaptation

Tone

Tone shifts based on context: celebratory and bold on marketing pages, warm and steady during planning conversations, tender and present in grief support.

To Normalize

Make death planning feel as natural as wedding planning. Remove stigma through casual confidence.

To Celebrate

Frame end-of-life planning as an act of love and self-expression, not morbid obligation.

To Empower

Give people agency over their own story. Your life, your party, your rules.

Be Clever

Headlines, taglines, social media, awareness campaigns — this is where personality lives loudest.

Be Clear

Service descriptions, pricing, planning guides, grief resources — clarity is kindness here.
